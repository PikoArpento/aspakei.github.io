History of the Empire - Chapter 6, The assassination of Prydon Vos

By 220 p.I., the Pact of Lucozhkei had created a fragile peace. However, tensions from the Second Big War persisted, especially among the defeated powers, Kojkos and Lamna were looking for ways to recover their prestige and influence. Meanwhile, Aspakei and Sark, began implementing defensive measures that raised alarm among their neighbours. The key points emerged in the Neutral Corridor, a territory left intentionally demilitarized under the treaty to serve as a zone between the warring nations. This corridor, though legally neutral, became a hotbed of espionage, smuggling, and political intrigue.

In late 219 p.I., tensions raised when Prydon Vos, a Sarkan diplomat, was assassinated in Mirconkei, a trade hub in the Neutral Corridor. The assassin, a known Lamnan nationalist who went by the name of Ýk Dada, was captured by Aspakeian intelligence agents. Lamna’s government immediately denied involvement, claiming the assassin was a lone wolf. Aspakei, however, released evidence linking the nationalist to a secret Lamnan paramilitary cell. Sark, outraged by the assassination, demanded reparations and threatened military action against Lamna. Kojkos, seeking to protect its ally, declared that any aggression against Lamna would be considered an attack on itself, raising the spectre of a new war.

In an effort to shift the blame, Lamna accused Aspakei of violating the treaty by operating spies in the Neutral Corridor. Kojkos stood with Lamna, citing “irrefutable evidence” of Aspakeian drone surveillance near its borders. Though Aspakei denied the allegations, it could not fully refute them, as its intelligence operations in the corridor were actually true. The diplomatic standoff paralyzed the region. Sark began mobilizing troops near the Neutral Corridor, while Kojkos and Lamna fortified their border defences. Aspakei, caught in the middle, tried to call for international support but found its credibility strained due to the covert nature of its operations.

In 218 p.I., Aspakei proposed an emergency summit in Kadekei to prevent the crisis from spiralling into open conflict. Representatives from all major powers attended, though tensions ran high. The situation worsened when a Lamnan reconnaissance plane was shot down over Aspakeian airspace, further escalating tensions. Lamna claimed it was an unarmed diplomatic flight, while Aspakei argued it was gathering intelligence on its defences. Faced with the imminent threat of war, Aspakei’s Senate leader, Fankasko Kalvoro Rosozhkeif, proposed the creation of an international framework that would render direct military aggression politically and economically unviable. This proposal, known as the Neutral Defence Doctrine (NDD), centred on three key principles: That any attack on another nation’s soil would automatically trigger a multi-state defensive alliance against the aggressor. An international coalition would enforce crippling economic sanctions on the aggressor, targeting trade, banking, and resources. And a Neutral Zone Security Force (NZSF) would be established. A peacekeeping force, drawn from every nation, would oversee the Neutral Corridor and other contested areas, ensuring compliance with the treaty.

The proposal faced resistance. Kojkos and Lamna argued that the doctrine would disproportionately benefit Aspakei and Sark by protecting their territorial gains from the Second Big War. However, smaller states, fearful of becoming battlegrounds in a larger conflict, united behind the NDD.

To silence sceptics, Aspakei agreed to submit its intelligence operations to oversight by the International Neutrality Council (INC), a body established to monitor compliance with the doctrine. This concession, along with Sark’s agreement to scale back its military presence near the Neutral Corridor, eventually convinced enough nations to support the NDD. In 217 p.I., the Kadekei Pact for Defence Doctrine was signed.

The NDD allowed nations to focus on internal development and technological advancement, while using the INC to diplomatically counteract its rivals for Aspakei. For Sark instead, the framework solidified its influence in the region without overextending its military. Finally, for Kojkos and Lamna, though they resented the doctrine, they were forced to comply to avoid economic isolation.
