History of the Empire - Chapter 3.4, The crisis after Torvoynko

The elections of 280 p.I. marked the republic’s return to democracy. The reformist coalition, now unified under the Democratic Restoration Party (DRP), achieved a decisive victory, bringing a new era of governance. Senator Karvo Iperbesto Gorkeif, a key figure in the resistance against Torvoynko, was inaugurated as president. His administration focused on rebuilding public trust in the government and enacting structural reforms to curb military influence.

One of the first measures taken was the disbanding of the Joint Security Council and the introduction of a constitutional amendment that explicitly limited military involvement in civilian affairs. A civilian oversight committee was created to supervise the military, and funds were redirected to enhance education, infrastructure, and healthcare, addressing the underlying issues that had led to unrest.

Investigations into the general’s inner circle uncovered a web of secret supporters who had profited during his rule. Numerous high-ranking officers and bureaucrats were implicated in corruption scandals, including the misappropriation of war funds and covert dealings with Sark.

These revelations ignited public outrage, prompting widespread demands for reformation. Karvo launched a series of trials and investigations by creating the Tribunal of Reckoning. While many appreciated the initiative to eliminate corruption, some thought that the trials could alienate segments of the population still loyal to Torvoynko’s legacy.

One such group, identifying themselves as the Iron Vanguard, began to organize in secrete. Comprised of former officers and disillusioned veterans, they positioned themselves as the true guardians of Aspakei’s honour. Their message began to resonate in rural areas that felt overlooked during the post-dictatorship recovery.

By 277 p.I., Aspakei had to deal with an increasing gap between urban centres and rural areas. While cities like Kadekei and Isozhkei thrived thanks to new infrastructure projects, the countryside was left with outdated facilities and few economic opportunities. Farmers, miners, and labourers began to express their frustrations, accusing the government of favouring urban elites over the essential workers of the republic. The Iron Vanguard took advantage of the situation, portraying the government as corrupt and ineffective. Their leaders called for a return to “Torvoynko’s principles,” which they argued had emphasized national strength and unity. Small protests escalated into larger rallies, and rumours started to circulate about armed training camps in remote areas. President Malor, recognizing the rising tensions, initiated a rural revitalization program, directing resources toward infrastructure and education in the countryside. However, progress was slow, and distrust between the government and rural communities continued to grow.

By 275 p.I., the Iron Vanguard had become a bigger threat. Intelligence reports indicated that the group had stockpiled weapons and gained support from factions within the military. Their propaganda portrayed the government as illegitimate, advocating for a “Second Restoration” to bring back Aspakei’s former glory. In response, the government designated the Iron Vanguard as a terrorist organization and activated the Aspakeian Intelligence Agency (AIA) to dismantle their operations. Raids on suspected hideouts revealed caches of weapons and encrypted communications, uncovering plans for coordinated attacks on key government facilities. On May 3, 274 p.I. a bomb exploded at the Kadekei Central Station, causing numerous casualties, and the Iron Vanguard claimed responsibility for the attack. President Malor declared the state of emergency, granting the government temporary powers to combat the insurgents.

Internationally, the situation raised alarms among Aspakei’s allies. Tehera and Mal provided logistical support and intelligence but urged Malor to seek a political resolution. However, the president remained firm in his belief that the Iron Vanguard posed an existential threat that could not be negotiated with.

As the conflict continued, public sentiment began to change. The extended state of emergency and reports of civilian casualties during military operations diminished support for the government. Protests erupted in Kadekei and other major cities, calling for an end to the violence and a return to normality. By late 273 p.I., President Malor faced increasing pressure to resign. His failure to resolve the insurgency and the growing authoritarian measures taken during the state of emergency alienated many of his previous supporters. Demands for new elections intensified, with reformists, moderates, and even some military leaders pushing for a change in leadership.

The military, concerned about a potential collapse of civilian authority, began to position itself as a possible mediator. General Lermo Fayorshao Kadekeif, an emerging figure in the army, made a public statement calling for unity and offering to help facilitate peace talks with the insurgents. However, many were worried because they thought that it might lead to another military takeover. By early 272 p.I., Aspakei found itself at a critical juncture. The insurgency continued to pose a significant threat, the government was fragmented, and the possibility of military intervention loomed once again.
