History of the Empire - Chapter 2.3, the First Big War

On October 24th, 330 p.I., on the 67th anniversary of independence, the annual elections were held. The «PIVA» Party, which stands for «Pederaco bu Istankastoyoizh Vamboizh Aspakeif», or «Federation of Aspakei Veterans», lost the elections with only 3% of the votes. Therefore, the next day their leader Shkom Arpentur Borvozhkeif led a march on Kadekei with a private militia. As PIVA was of Elu origin, it was supported by powerful nations like the Kingdom of Sark and the Democratic Republic of Xam’apo. The army of Aspakei was mobilized to stop the march, and the afternoon was marked by a series of battles between the two military forces in the city centre. Aspakei emerged victorious, and all participants were arrested.

Aspakei's response was not well received by Elu's allies, who demanded that they be put back in power. Obviously Aspakei refused, so on January 1, 329 p.I., the Democratic Republic of Xam'apo deployed troops to the border. Aspakei immediately called for an emergency meeting with the other republics in the Treaty. Meanwhile, Aspakei drones flew over the border, revealing continuous troop movements, and, most importantly, the construction of field hospitals. Aspakei also deployed troops 10 km from the border. Meanwhile, the Republic of Tehera sent armed support to Aspakei and some intelligence agents to investigate in enemy territory. On February 3, Aspakei ambassador Paxur Rospur Isozhkeif, contacts the President of Xam'apo, to ask their intentions. The answer is simple and direct, and Xam'apo declares war.

As the war between Aspakei and the Democratic Republic of Xam’apo broke out, Aspakei strategists focused their efforts on securing the Geshbo, a fertile region critical for maintaining supply lines and housing the dense network of industrial hubs vital to the republic’s war machine. Xam’apo, aware of the region’s significance, launched coordinated assaults from the west, hoping to fracture the defensive line. The first skirmishes in mid-February were fierce but inconclusive, with both sides suffering heavy casualties. By late February, Aspakei forces had fortified a stronghold along Malnoro ridge, overlooking the Geshbo. The ridge’s natural elevation provided a strategic advantage, allowing Aspakei artillery to rain down on advancing Xam’apo battalions. However, Xam’apo deployed specialized tunnelling units, attempting to undermine the ridge’s defences. The ensuing battle, dubbed the “Siege of the Malnoro Tunnels,” lasted 16 days and ended in an Aspakei victory, solidifying their hold over the basin.

In March, Xam’apo shifted its focus westward to the Ulgo Plains, hoping to open a second front and stretch Aspakei forces thin. The flat terrain favoured Xam’apo’s mechanized divisions, and their rapid armoured thrusts initially overwhelmed Aspakei’s infantry. By mid-April, Xam’apo forces had seized several critical towns along the plains, prompting the Republic of Tehera to commit additional reinforcements. Tehera’s elite cavalry and Aspakei’s innovative use of drone surveillance turned the tide. A coordinated counteroffensive in early May led to the decisive Battle of Dorvo Crossroads, where Xam’apo’s armoured columns were encircled and destroyed. The victory secured the Ulgo Plains for Aspakei and its allies, but at the cost of significant manpower and resources, leaving both sides locked in a stalemate. While battles raged on the front lines, diplomatic efforts continued behind the scenes. The Republic of Mal, initially hesitant to commit significant resources, began playing a more active role, offering logistical support and brokering secret negotiations with neutral states to prevent the war from expanding further. Meanwhile, the Kingdom of Sark and its allies escalated their support for Xam’apo, funnelling weapons and supplies across the northern seas.

By early August, there were whispers of a potential armistice, but mistrust between the warring factions remained high. Aspakei’s leaders demanded the withdrawal of all Xam’apo forces and recognition of their sovereignty, while Xam’apo insisted on reparations and concessions for the Elu populations within Aspakei’s borders. the basin.

In September, Aspakei launched Operation Iron Wave, a bold offensive designed to push Xam’apo’s forces out of the contested territories entirely. Combining precision drone strikes, coordinated infantry assaults, and a strategic naval blockade in the Bay of Zhero, the operation achieved remarkable success. Xam’apo’s supply lines were severed, forcing their troops into a retreat. Despite the military victories, the strain on Aspakei’s population and resources was immense. Civilian morale wavered under the constant pressure of wartime rationing and mounting casualties. Yet, the victories on the battlefield reignited a sense of unity and purpose, strengthening the resolve of the republic to endure.

By October, both sides were exhausted, and calls for peace grew louder. A temporary ceasefire was declared on October 21, and delegates from Aspakei, Xam’apo, and their respective allies gathered in neutral Tehera for peace talks. The negotiations were tense and fraught with accusations, but the horrors of prolonged conflict compelled both sides to compromise.

On December 12, the Treaty of Zhero was signed, officially ending the war. Xam’apo agreed to withdraw its forces and acknowledge Aspakei’s sovereignty, while Aspakei made concessions to ensure the cultural rights of the Elu population. Though tensions lingered, the treaty marked the beginning of a fragile peace, ushering in a new era of cautious cooperation and rebuilding.
